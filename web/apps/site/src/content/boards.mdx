# Choosing a development board

Teslasynth runs on any ESP32, ESP32-S2, or ESP32-S3 dev board with at least
4 MB of flash. But not every board is equally pleasant to work with — this
page lists what to look for and what to avoid.

## What to look for

**A USB-to-UART bridge chip.** Boards with a CP210x, CH340, or FT232 between
the chip and the USB connector flash reliably from the [web installer](/) and
have predictable auto-reset. Avoid boards where the chip's native USB pins
(D+/D−) are wired straight to the USB connector — they require manual
button presses or a workaround for every flash.

**At least 5 usable GPIO pins.** Teslasynth uses 4 output channels plus 1
optional status LED by default. Make sure the board exposes that many pins
that aren't strapping pins, aren't reserved for the on-board USB-UART, and
aren't already wired to a built-in LED, button, or peripheral.

**3.3 V logic.** All ESP32 family chips are 3.3 V. Confirm your interrupter
input accepts 3.3 V logic, or plan for a level shifter / opto-isolator
between the board and your high-voltage hardware.

**Through-hole pin headers.** Easier to wire to interrupter boards and
breadboards than tiny castellated edge pads.

## What to skip

- **Boards with native-USB-only wiring** (no UART bridge IC). The web
  installer can't put these into download mode — see the
  [troubleshooting note](/getting-started#esp32-s2-boards-with-native-usb-s2-mini-s2-saola).
  ESP32-S2-Mini and S2-Saola fall into this category.
- **2 MB flash variants.** The firmware fits but leaves no room for OTA
  updates or future growth.
- **Modules with sub-20 exposed pins.** Once you subtract strapping pins,
  USB pins, and the boot/reset buttons, you may not have enough free GPIOs.

## Connection options recap

See the [main page](/) for the connection options table per chip family
(USB MIDI vs Bluetooth MIDI). USB MIDI is recommended for any serious coil
ensemble — Bluetooth introduces jitter on fast note runs and is unreliable
near strong electric fields.
